TL;DR — the whole thing in plain language

Let doctors contribute knowledge — and let a machine check it — before anyone trusts it.

Doctors hold most of the useful knowledge in medicine, but there is no easy, trustworthy way to put a clinical rule, a case pattern, or an outcome observation into a shared system that others can use, check and build on. Meanwhile AI can draft clinical text fast — but clinicians rightly distrust anything they cannot verify. VGC-Health joins the two: a clinician contributes in plain language, and nothing is accepted until an independent gate (guidelines, outcome data, a second reviewer) confirms it. It extends a proven software method, VGC, into healthcare.

  • Contribute without coding. State a rule or observation in your own words; the system does the rest.
  • Nothing is trusted until it's checked. Every entry passes a multi-oracle gate, or is honestly marked "can't verify yet" — never silently accepted.
  • Three axes that never collapse. Every entry is tagged by domain (mind / body / society), care setting (clinic → ICU → hospice → home) and system (records, claims, trials, devices) — so it's findable from all three.
  • One manifold, the whole spectrum. Specialties & superspecialties, day-care to hospice, hospital management, epidemiology, drug & device trials, HTA, medical robotics, billing & insurance — each a filtered view of one gated source, not a new silo.
  • Accountability is built in. A named clinician signs off before anything touches real care; that signature is recorded immutably (the Regulatory Provenance Ledger).
  • Start small, prove it, then widen. A tiny pilot: one protocol, 3–5 clinicians, decision-support only, no live patients — measured on how fast contributions are gated and how often reviewers agree.
  • Honest limits. Good clinical oracles are hard to build; adoption isn't guaranteed. This is a proposal to be tested and, if wrong, falsified.

The full proposal · for readers who want the detail

Abstract

Modern language models can draft clinical text and code quickly, yet clinicians rightly distrust output they cannot verify, and the health system remains fragmented: practice, research, education and outcomes sit in silos, and most of the richest patient information — the physician's own notes and reasoning — never becomes shared, structured, checkable knowledge. We propose VGC-Health, a direct extension of Verification-Gated Human–AI Co-Development (VGC)[1] — a methodology in which no generated artifact is accepted until an independent, machine-checkable oracle confirms it — from software into clinical knowledge. The central move is to let a clinician contribute by stating intent in natural language and to route every contribution through a multi-oracle gate before it enters a shared, versioned knowledge manifold. We redefine VGC's six collaboration primitives for a clinical setting, add a healthcare-specific primitive — a Regulatory Provenance Ledger recording which named, accountable clinician approved which version — and specify a small, falsifiable pilot with pre-registered metrics. The same manifold, queried along three non-collapsing axes (domain, care-setting, system), serves specialties and superspecialties, day-care through hospice, hospital management, epidemiology, drug-trial support, health-technology assessment, medical devices and robotics, and billing/insurance — and slots into national digital-health infrastructure rather than replacing it. Threats to validity are stated candidly. The transferable contribution is not any single clinical rule; it is the gate, and a contribution path a clinician will actually use.

1The problem: knowledge that never becomes shared knowledge

Contemporary digital-health work, in India and elsewhere, has made real progress on the lower layers: coded lab data, imaging informatics and medication data are increasingly standardised, national digital-health platforms and identity spines exist, and interoperability standards are maturing. Yet the reported bottlenecks are consistent, and not primarily technical. In an ongoing practitioner research discussion on the state of digital health and AI, a clinician-researcher framed the gaps precisely: the richest patient information — physician notes and clinical reasoning, on the order of two-thirds of the useful signal — is still poorly captured; interoperability is operationally hard even where standards are clear; there is little urgency around converting clinical data into clinical intelligence; delivery, research, education and outcome research remain siloed; and there is no alignment between the clinical ecosystem and the insurance and regulatory bodies who could reduce the cost of care through scientific, informatics-driven claims processing. The same discussion stressed that clinical explainability, clearly defined intelligence objectives, data literacy among clinicians, a clinical data architecture complementing the IT one, and a larger pool of clinical data scientists are the levers — and that data in silos always yields sub-optimal outputs.

We take these observations as the design brief. The common thread is a missing contribution mechanism: no low-friction, trust-preserving way for a clinician to move a piece of their own judgment into a shared, structured, checkable form that research, education, outcomes work and downstream systems (claims, HTA, trials) can all draw on. More storage does not fix this; the manifold is not a database, it is a gated contribution loop.

▸ Technical detail — why this is a knowledge-representation problem, not a storage problem optional

A relational or FHIR-native store answers "what happened to this patient?" It does not answer "what does the profession collectively believe, at version v, about pattern P — and what evidence gates that belief?" The latter is a manifold in the informal sense we use: a structured space of assertions, each carrying (i) a natural-language statement, (ii) an axis-tagged position, (iii) a verification record, (iv) a provenance/accountability record, and (v) a version lineage under exact-anchor revision. FHIR resources remain the substrate the manifold reads from and writes decision-support into; the manifold is the epistemic layer above it. This keeps clinical assertions interoperable-by-reference without conflating "a fact about a patient" with "a gated belief of the profession."

2Background: what VGC is, and why it transfers

Verification-Gated Human–AI Co-Development[1] is a loop in which a senior human directs an AI executor and acceptance belongs to a machine-checkable oracle, never to either party. Its load-bearing innovation is a cross-representation parity oracle: an independent second implementation, in a different language with different failure modes, checked field-by-field against the reference — converting "the AI probably got it right" into thousands of passing assertions. The published case study (CHAKRA, an offline astronomical/calendrical engine) reported a 74-file, six-language system verified continuously by a 120-assertion suite and a 6,590-check parity harness, built for a small fraction of conventional cost. The paper's own honest caveat is the one that matters most here: VGC's leverage is domain-shaped — it excels where correctness is checkable, and degrades "where the oracle is as hard to build as the system." Healthcare is exactly such a hard-oracle domain — which is why the health version needs a different, multi-part gate rather than a single arithmetic one.

The core shift

CHAKRA's oracle was cheap: astronomy is right to arc-minutes or wrong. Healthcare has no single arithmetic oracle — correctness is at once clinical, evidential, regulatory and probabilistic. So VGC-Health replaces the one parity harness with a stack of parallel oracles that must each pass, and treats "no oracle available" as an explicit, tracked state rather than a silent acceptance.

3The health manifold: three axes that must never collapse

A recurring failure in health IT is axis collapse — folding a rich, multi-dimensional clinical assertion into a single flat category (a form field, an app screen) and losing the other dimensions along which it is useful. VGC-Health forbids this structurally: every entry is positioned on three orthogonal axes, and queries filter along any combination.

Axis 1 · Domain
Mind · Body · Society
  • Mind — psychiatry, neurology, palliative psychology, cognition
  • Body — all organ-system specialties & superspecialties
  • Society — epidemiology, public & population health, HTA
Axis 2 · Care setting
Where care happens
  • Outpatient / day-care
  • Inpatient / ward / ICU
  • Hospice & palliative
  • Home & community care
Axis 3 · System
What it touches
  • HMS / EHR workflows
  • Insurance & claims logic
  • Drug-trial protocols
  • Device & robotics interfaces

The axes are independent coordinates, not a hierarchy. A single assertion — say, a drug-interaction rule in a psychiatric inpatient on a specific claims pathway — is one entry, findable from all three directions, rather than three disconnected records.

▸ Technical detail — entry schema and enforced orthogonality optional

Each entry is a tuple \(\;e = \langle s,\vec{a},V,L,\pi \rangle\): \(s\) natural-language statement; \(\vec{a}=(a_1,a_2,a_3)\) the axis position; \(V\) the verification record (which oracles ran, outputs, pass/defer/fail); \(L\) the version lineage (parent + exact-anchor diff); \(\pi\) the provenance/accountability record. Orthogonality is enforced at contribution time: the UI requires an explicit position on all three axes and rejects entries that encode a second axis inside the first. Queries are set operations over axis coordinates, so the same entry is reachable independently by domain, by setting, and by system. No axis is ever the "primary key" — this is what stops the manifold degenerating into another siloed EHR feature.

4The contribution loop: VGC's primitives, redefined for clinicians

VGC's six primitives were written for code. Below, each is restated for a clinical contributor, preserving the essential property — acceptance belongs to an oracle, not the author — while replacing the software-specific oracle with clinically appropriate ones. A seventh, healthcare-specific primitive is added.

Primitive H1 · from VGC P1

Multi-Oracle Gate core

was: single cross-representation parity oracle

A contribution must pass several independent checks in parallel: (a) guideline conformance against current, coded clinical-practice-guideline text (retrieved live, not recalled); (b) an outcome-regression check against known-ground-truth data where it exists; (c) a second-clinician review for anything without hard data. Where logic is deterministic — dosage, eligibility, coding — the original parity idea still applies: a second independent implementation pinned against the first.

Primitive H2 · from VGC P2

Exact-Anchor Revision auditability

was: exact-anchor patching of source files

An update must cite exactly which prior assertion and version it revises; whole-entry regeneration is forbidden, and if the anchor isn't uniquely found the revision aborts. Silent drift in a dosing rule is a patient-safety event, not a merge conflict.

Primitive H3 · from VGC P3

Clinical Guarded Irreversibility safety

was: dry-run-by-default for DOI/push/delete

Anything touching PHI, a prescription, a record, or a claim defaults to simulation and touches nothing live. Promotion to a real action requires explicit escalation and sign-off by a named, accountable clinician — not merely a typed confirmation. Data-protection duties are a hard gate here, not a footnote.

Primitive H4 · from VGC P4

Source-of-Truth Reconciliation coherence

was: re-adopt canonical repo, diff to zero

The canonical manifold is re-adopted at the start of any work, verifying agreement rather than assuming it — extended to reconciling against the current version of the relevant guideline or interaction database, because protocols update in ways constants never do.

Primitive H5 · from VGC P5

Clinical-Precedent Grounding anti-hallucination

was: read the existing code before rebuilding it

Before generating any clinical logic, the system retrieves the actual current guideline/protocol text, not the model's memorised version — countering the dominant AI failure mode (confidently reinventing something that already exists, subtly and dangerously differently) and making explainability concrete.

Primitive H6 · from VGC P6

Honest Deferral scope integrity

was: tracked backlog of unverified work

Anything the gate cannot verify is logged as an open, tracked issue — never faked, stubbed, or shipped as "done." Silently-dropped uncertainty is the dangerous failure mode; here it is made visible by construction.

Primitive H7 · new in VGC-Health

Regulatory Provenance Ledger new

Every accepted entry records which named clinician/reviewer approved which version, when. In software, "the oracle decided" suffices; in medicine a licensed professional is legally accountable, so the ledger records human approval as a first-class, immutable, timestamped fact — the auditable spine that lets the manifold be used in regulated settings without pretending the machine bears responsibility.

▸ Technical detail — the loop as a state machine, and the "no-oracle" state optional

The loop is intent → elicitation → oracle-stack → {accept | defer | repair}. An entry \(e\) enters stack \(\Omega=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_k\}\); acceptance requires \(\bigwedge_i \omega_i(e)=\text{pass}\) for all oracles applicable to \(e\)'s axis position. Applicability is explicit: an assertion with no ground-truth data has \(\omega_\text{outcome}=\)N/A, and if the remaining oracles can't decide, \(e\) enters the Open — Honest Deferral state rather than accept. The manifold thus has a first-class "we don't yet know" value it never collapses into "true." Promotion from accept-as-knowledge to act-on-patient is a separate transition gated by H3 + H7, requiring a named clinician signature in the append-only ledger; no AI transition crosses this boundary autonomously — it is structural, not advisory.

5The proposed experiment (pilot design)

The experiment is deliberately narrow and falsifiable — an existence proof that the contribution loop works before any attempt to scale.

5.1 Hypothesis

A clinician who does not write code can contribute a clinical rule, case pattern, or outcome observation such that it is (i) gated against evidence and precedent, (ii) accepted, rejected, or honestly deferred with a legible rationale, and (iii) revised only by exact-anchor diff — and that this loop achieves useful inter-reviewer agreement at a contribution latency clinicians will tolerate.

▸ Technical detail — reference architecture & oracle stack optional

Substrate. The manifold reads from and writes decision-support into a FHIR-native record layer; it does not replace it. Coded terminologies (ICD/SNOMED-class) anchor axis-1 positions and guideline lookups. Oracle stack (H1). \(\omega_\text{guideline}\): retrieval-grounded conformance against the current CPG version; \(\omega_\text{outcome}\): regression against held-out labelled cases where available; \(\omega_\text{review}\): blinded second-clinician adjudication; \(\omega_\text{parity}\): for deterministic sub-logic (dose, eligibility, coding), a second independent implementation pinned field-by-field. Explainability. Every accepted entry auto-emits a plain-language rationale trace naming the oracles that ran, the precedent grounded against, and the reviewer(s) of record. Ledger (H7). Append-only, hash-chained, independently exportable.

6One manifold, the whole spectrum

Because contributions are axis-tagged rather than app-bound, the wider health system consumes the same accepted, evidenced entries through different filters:

Table 3 · The manifold as a shared source for the full spectrum of health work

Why this addresses the silo problem directly

Claims processing gaining a citable clinical source is what could finally align the clinical, insurance and regulatory ecosystems around one evidenced substrate. The scarce clinical-data-scientist talent concentrates at the gate/oracle layer, not at the point of clinical input — so clinicians contribute without needing that skillset, which is what makes broad participation feasible at all.

7Governance, ethics & regulatory posture

The governance model is not layered on top; it is the primitives. H3 makes patient-facing action structurally impossible without named sign-off; H7 records that sign-off immutably; H6 makes uncertainty visible; H4/H5 keep the manifold grounded in current, real guidance. The pilot runs on retrospective or synthetic cases, exposing no protected health information until governance is demonstrated. VGC-Health is positioned as a consumer of and contributor to national digital-health infrastructure (identity spine, FHIR interoperability) — an epistemic layer above the substrate, not a competing platform.

8Threats to validity

  • The oracle is the hard part. VGC's own caveat applies with full force: in domains where the oracle is as hard to build as the system, the gate degrades. Much of medicine is such a domain. The multi-oracle stack is a mitigation, not a guarantee; where no oracle exists, the honest answer is deferral.
  • Adoption risk. The premise is that clinicians will contribute in natural language. If elicitation friction is too high, or the gate feels adversarial, the loop stalls — which is why v0 measures latency and reviewer-agreement as primary endpoints.
  • Single narrow pilot. One protocol, a few clinicians, no live patients. Results are an existence proof of the mechanism, not a population claim about clinical benefit.
  • Second-reviewer scarcity. H1(c) leans on a second clinician; in under-resourced settings that reviewer may not exist — itself a measured constraint rather than an assumed input.
  • Author as proposer. This is a design proposal by the methodology's author; independent replication and re-estimation are explicitly invited.

9Call to clinicians & health professionals

The contribution path is built around clinical judgment, not technical skill: you state a rule, pattern, or observation in your own words; the gate checks it and returns a legible verdict; you revise by pointing at exactly what changed. The scarce engineering sits at the oracle layer, out of your way. If you are a physician, nurse, allied-health professional, epidemiologist, HTA analyst, or device/robotics clinician-engineer and this loop is one you would use, this proposal is an open invitation to help build and stress-test the first narrow slice — and to falsify it if it does not hold. Try the Prompt Studio above on a problem you know. The idea is to discuss, not to debate.
