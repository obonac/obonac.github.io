# Why this document exists

Most biographies describe a person.

This document instead attempts to describe a **knowledge system**.

The distinction is fundamental.

A conventional biography assumes a person progresses through life as follows:

```
Education

↓

Job

↓

Promotion

↓

Leadership

↓

Recognition
```

The resulting document becomes a curriculum vitae.

That model works reasonably well for professionals whose work largely follows institutional structures.

It fails almost completely when the principal artefact is neither employment nor academic appointment, but a long-running, self-evolving multidisciplinary research programme.

This document argues that the work documented here is better understood as an evolving **knowledge architecture** than as a sequence of jobs.

---

# The Accounting Problem

During the preparation of this work, an interesting observation emerged.

When asked

> **"Were the first thirty-one years worthwhile?"**

the immediate instinct—even for an AI trained on millions of biographies—was to evaluate the period using familiar measures:

* promotions,
* titles,
* publications,
* awards,
* employers,
* citations.

That accounting repeatedly underestimated the evidence.

The error was not factual.

The error was structural.

The accounting model itself was incorrect.

# Why conventional CVs fail

Suppose a curriculum vitae contains

```
MA Psychology

MA Linguistics

MA Philosophy

MBA

Systems Architect

IBM

HP

Research

Open Source
```

To most readers this appears as a list.

To the author of the work, however, those are not independent entries.

They are interacting systems.

Psychology influences learning.

Learning influences educational technology.

Educational technology influences artificial intelligence.

Artificial intelligence influences healthcare.

Healthcare influences medical devices.

Medical devices influence robotics.

Robotics influences distributed infrastructure.

Distributed infrastructure influences virtualisation.

Virtualisation influences AI infrastructure.

Programming languages influence nearly everything.

A CV destroys those relationships.

The graph disappears.

# Why chronology matters

A second problem with conventional biographies is historical flattening.

A statement such as

> "Developed a wearable medical device."

appears ordinary when read in 2026.

It is not ordinary when placed in its historical context.

Likewise

* autonomous robotics in 2003,
* multilingual programming systems in 2004,
* registry-driven architectures years before they became common,
* early learning automata,
* or long-running work on multilingual computing

must be interpreted relative to the technologies available at the time.

Chronology is therefore not decorative.

Chronology is evidence.

# The Repository Fallacy

At the time of writing this document, the public ecosystem contains approximately

* more than one hundred GitHub organisations,
* more than four hundred public repositories,
* multiple project websites,
* architectural documentation,
* books,
* white papers,
* technical notes,
* historical screenshots,
* research artefacts,
* educational material,
* and software spanning several decades.

The obvious mistake is to interpret these as hundreds of unrelated software projects.

Repository archaeology conducted during the preparation of this work suggests the opposite.

The repositories increasingly appear to be manifestations of a much smaller number of long-running research programmes.

Examples include:

* Hindawi Programming System
* Project ILM
* AyeVDI
* AyeLearn
* ValidCV
* AyeHandsum
* Healthcare ecosystems
* Medical specialty ecosystems
* Educational systems
* AI infrastructure
* Robotics
* Linguistic computing

Each programme evolved over many years and frequently reused architectural ideas developed in earlier decades.

The repositories therefore document evolution rather than fragmentation.

# Why multidisciplinary is an insufficient description

Throughout this document the word **multidisciplinary** will be used sparingly.

The term has become so common that it has almost lost meaning.

Many researchers collaborate across disciplines.

The work described here is different.

The disciplines themselves repeatedly merge into single systems.

Examples include:

Psychology becoming software architecture.

Linguistics becoming compiler engineering.

Philosophy becoming ontology.

Healthcare becoming distributed systems.

Education becoming artificial intelligence.

Programming languages becoming instruments of linguistic equity.

These are not collaborations between disciplines.

They are architectural integrations.

# Enterprise and Independent Research

Another false dichotomy frequently appears in biographies.

Work is divided into

* employment
* personal work

This document rejects that division.

Professional engineering contributed directly to independent research.

Independent research influenced enterprise thinking.

Enterprise experience improved later open-source architecture.

Open-source work informed later research.

The boundary is therefore permeable rather than absolute.

# Beyond Software

Although much of the visible evidence is software, reducing the body of work to software engineering would be another accounting error.

The documented programme includes sustained work involving:

* systems engineering,
* artificial intelligence,
* healthcare,
* robotics,
* medical devices,
* psychology,
* linguistics,
* philosophy,
* education,
* entrepreneurship,
* competency systems,
* multilingual computing,
* evidence architectures,
* and social initiatives concerned with technological accessibility and linguistic equity.

Software became the medium through which many of these ideas were expressed.

It was not always the objective.

# What follows

The chapters that follow do not attempt to establish greatness.

Nor do they attempt to market accomplishments.

Instead they pursue a narrower objective:

To reconstruct, as faithfully as possible, the architecture of a thirty-one-year multidisciplinary programme whose visible artefacts include enterprise engineering, research, software, education, writing, entrepreneurship and public technical ecosystems.

Only after reconstructing that architecture does it become meaningful to ask questions about productivity, impact or significance.

Anything less risks repeating the same accounting error that motivated the writing of this document.
